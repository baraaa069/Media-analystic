DUNE: PART TWO

A media literacy analysis of the TV spot and movie poster

Explore Analysis

Critical Analysis

TV Commercial

Video

Q1. Who made this message and what is the purpose?

The TV spot was created by Warner Bros. Pictures' marketing team with input from director Denis Villeneuve. Its primary purpose is to generate maximum excitement for the theatrical release by showcasing the most spectacular elements while avoiding spoilers. Secondary purposes include reinforcing the Dune brand and differentiating this sequel from other sci-fi films.

Q2. Which techniques are used to attract your attention?

Visual

  • Rapid cuts (24 shots in 30 seconds) create urgency
  • High contrast between dark interiors and bright desert
  • Spectacular VFX reveals (sandworm emergence)
  • Close-ups of recognizable stars (Chalamet, Zendaya)

Audio

  • Epic choir vocals in soundtrack
  • Booming bass effects (sandworm roar at 0:22)
  • Authoritative voiceover: "The battle for Arrakis begins..."
  • Strategic silence before dramatic moments

Theatrical Poster

Image

Q3. What meaning does this message have? How might others interpret it differently?

My interpretation: The poster presents Paul Atreides as a messianic figure through its symmetrical composition and golden hues, suggesting his central role in the prophecy. The vast desert emphasizes the harshness of Arrakis.

Alternative interpretations: Some might see the poster as promoting a white savior narrative (Paul as the dominant central figure), while others could interpret the small human figures as commentary on humanity's insignificance against nature.

Q4. What point of view is represented?

  • Studio perspective: Highlights bankable stars (Chalamet's face most visible)
  • Designer perspective: Focuses on creating an iconic, minimalist image
  • Cultural perspective: Western cinematic traditions of hero positioning
  • Missing perspectives: Indigenous Fremen viewpoints, female characters' roles

Message Creation

Warner Bros. marketing team analyzed audience data from Part One to emphasize action sequences and star power in the TV spot, while the poster was designed by Legion Creative Group to create an instantly recognizable visual identity.

Attention Techniques

The commercial uses sensory overload (rapid visuals, deep bass) while the poster employs negative space and color contrast - demonstrating how different media require different approaches to capture attention.

Diverse Interpretations

While I see the poster as artistic, a marketing executive might view it as a calculated product. A non-fan might mistake it for a Middle Eastern war film rather than sci-fi.

Omitted Elements

Neither medium shows the complex political machinations, the 2h46m runtime, or the philosophical themes that dominate much of the actual film.

"All media messages are constructed with particular purposes using creative techniques. The smart viewer asks who made this, why, and what's being left out."
- Center for Media Literacy

Q5. What's missing from these messages?

Critical Insight

TV Spot Omissions

  • No indication of the film's slow, meditative pacing
  • Minimal dialogue - can't assess writing quality
  • Only 2 female characters shown despite important roles
  • No content warnings for violence

Poster Omissions

  • "Part Two" is de-emphasized (risking confusion)
  • No sense of the ensemble cast's importance
  • Doesn't convey the film's hybrid genre (war/politics/ecology)
  • Lacks any critical reviews or ratings

Key Takeaways

Media Literacy Insights

This analysis revealed three crucial media literacy principles:

  • Construction: Both pieces carefully construct reality - the TV spot exaggerates action, while the poster simplifies complex themes
  • Audience Targeting: The commercial targets general audiences, while the poster appeals to fans and art-house viewers
  • Omission as Message: What's excluded (female characters, runtime) communicates values as much as what's included

By applying the five critical questions, we can enjoy media while understanding its persuasive techniques.
